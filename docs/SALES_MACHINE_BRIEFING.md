# Sales Machine Briefing

Context doc for building website copy and landing pages that these outbound emails point to.

## What the Sales Machine Is

An automated agent that runs every weekday morning on Chris's Mac. It discovers organizations with active media/communications needs, researches them deeply, finds decision-maker contacts, writes personalized cold emails, and pushes them to Instantly CRM for delivery. Chris gets a briefing email each morning with everything it did.

The whole thing runs unattended. Chris reviews the briefing, spots-checks the auto-sent emails, and manually reviews anything the system held for quality concerns.

## How Prospects Get Discovered

Two sources, running in parallel:

1. **SAM.gov** - Federal RFPs for video production, media, communications, and creative services
2. **Google News via Claude web search** - 33 keyword queries covering Arizona government, healthcare, tribal, defense, nonprofit, education, real estate, tech, and national verticals

The agent scores every opportunity on a 1-10 warmth scale. Only 7+ make it through. Warmth means: this org has an active need right now, not "they might need video someday."

## What Happens Before the Email Gets Written

For every qualifying org, the agent runs deep research:

- **What they do** - mission, size, public presence
- **Recent activity** - news, hires, campaigns, funding, controversy
- **Visual storytelling gap** - where their current media/content is falling short
- **Why Thrill Wave** - specific angle for how we'd help this org specifically
- **Decision makers** - names, titles, emails (verified through Hunter.io)

This research is what makes the emails specific. The agent doesn't blast templates. It studies the org first, then writes.

## The Email

The email is short. Three parts:

### 1. Subject line
A 3-7 word observational fragment. Lowercase, no punctuation. Reads like a note someone jotted to themselves.

Examples:
- "one thing about Operation Not Forgotten"
- "the Mesa story's missing piece"
- "the recruitment blind spot"

### 2. Hook
One paragraph in Chris's voice that names a specific trigger event and delivers a concrete insight about a gap in the org's current communications/media. Uses one of four conversational scaffolds:

- "Watching [initiative] unfold and one piece keeps nagging at me..."
- "Quick thing on [initiative] that I don't think is getting enough airtime..."
- "Not sure if this is on your radar, but..."
- "Your team's probably already clocked this, but..."

The hook is the whole pitch. It demonstrates that someone actually looked at this org and found something real. Not a generic "you could benefit from video." A specific observation about a specific gap.

### 3. Bridge + CTA
Static line, same on every email:

> We specialize in this. Researching where the story's falling short and building the media that fixes it.
>
> Worth 20 minutes?

Then Chris's name, Thrill Wave, website, phone number.

## What the Prospect Experiences

1. They get a short, specific email that shows someone actually studied their org
2. The email mentions thrillwave.com
3. If they're curious, they visit the site
4. **This is where the website needs to pick up the thread**

## What the Website Needs to Do

The email makes one promise: "we researched where your story's falling short and we build the media that fixes it." The website has to back that up.

A prospect arriving from one of these emails already knows:
- Someone identified a specific gap in their communications
- That person works at Thrill Wave
- Thrill Wave does research first, then builds media

They do NOT know:
- What Thrill Wave actually produces (documentary, video, photography)
- What SITREP is
- How the process works
- What it costs
- Who else has worked with Thrill Wave

The website needs to answer all of that without losing the tone of the email. The email is peer-to-peer. Confident, specific, not salesy. The website can't suddenly turn into a capabilities deck.

## SITREP - The Intelligence Product

SITREP is Thrill Wave's OSINT-based brand intelligence report. This is the thing that makes the emails possible and the thing that differentiates Thrill Wave from every other production company.

**What it does:**
- External scan: social sentiment, search behavior, news coverage, competitor positioning, Reddit/YouTube/forum commentary around the org
- Internal layer (under NDA): stakeholder interviews, internal comms, strategic docs

**What it produces:**
The gap between how the world sees the org and how the org sees itself. That gap becomes the creative brief for everything Thrill Wave produces.

**How it's sold:**
- Standalone: $5K-$10K for the intelligence brief alone
- Bundled: as the discovery phase of a larger production contract

**Why it matters for the website:**
SITREP is the entry point. The email demonstrates SITREP thinking (we found a gap). The website should make the prospect want to see the full version. "You just got a taste of what we found. Imagine what a full SITREP looks like."

## Thrill Wave Identity

Three pillars: **Research. Intelligence. Film.**

- Documentary-style video production (drone, cable cam, high-speed, the real stuff)
- Journalistic photography
- Animated video
- Hybrid projects combining all three

Everything is grounded in research first. The positioning is: every production company asks "what do you want to make?" Nobody asks "what should you make?" That gap is where wasted budget lives.

## What Thrill Wave is NOT

- Not a "video production company" (never use this phrase)
- Not a PR firm
- Not a content mill, social media agency, or event videography outfit
- Not a tech company that happens to make video

## Copy Rules

These apply to the emails and should carry through to the website:

- Lead with the insight, not the AI. The intelligence layer is a capability, not the headline.
- Never use "content." Use media, film, video, or story.
- Never say a prospect is "failing"
- Don't promise ROI
- Don't lead with technology
- No em dashes in headlines
- Brand aesthetic: retro sci-fi, analog warmth, CRT-in-a-dark-room feeling
