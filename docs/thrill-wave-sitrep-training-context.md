## The Thesis

Your audience is already in a conversation. They have opinions, concerns, and things they're paying attention to right now. Your video either enters that conversation or it gets ignored. We study the conversation before we show up with a camera.

---

## The Problem We Solve

Most video fails because the story was wrong, not because the production was bad.

Every production company asks "what do you want to make?" Nobody asks "what should you make?" That gap is where all the wasted budget lives. Marketing teams run on vibes. They make the safe, obvious thing that checks a box. Meanwhile the most interesting stories inside the organization never make it into the media.

### How it breaks down

**Nobody did the homework.** The production company asked what the client wanted. They didn't ask what the audience actually needed to hear. The story was based on internal assumptions, not external reality.

**The signals were ignored.** What the audience says about the organization in reviews, comments, search queries, and forums tells you exactly what story to tell. Nobody looked. They brainstormed instead.

**Vibes replaced strategy.** The creative direction was chosen because it felt right, looked cool, or matched a trend. No evidence connected the concept to something the audience actually cares about.

## The Mechanism: SITREP

SITREP is a pre-production intelligence process. It has two layers.

### Layer 1: External (OSINT)

Open Source Intelligence. What the public internet says about the client. Publicly available information, systematically collected and analyzed by AI-powered research agents.

**Data sources:**

- Reddit threads and comments
- YouTube comments on competitor and industry content
- Glassdoor and Indeed reviews (sentiment trends over time)
- Google autocomplete, "People Also Ask," search intent data
- TikTok and Instagram comments on industry content
- News articles and comment sections
- Google Maps / Yelp reviews
- Local vs. national news coverage gaps
- Patent filings, grant applications, government contracts
- Job postings as strategic signals
- Founder/CEO personal social media vs. corporate accounts

**What the agents surface:**

- How the public describes the client vs. how the client describes itself
- Recurring sentiment themes (praise, complaints, confusion)
- Real language people use when talking about the industry
- Competitive perception
- The negative space: what the client isn't talking about that they should be
- Search behavior revealing audience confusion or curiosity

This layer can run before the client is engaged. It's the basis for the paid discovery product ($5K–$10K).

### Layer 2: Internal (Proprietary)

What the organization knows about itself that isn't public. Requires client access under NDA.

Described in organization-agnostic terms: the information the organization already has but hasn't connected to its story yet. What they know internally that the outside world doesn't see. Institutional knowledge, feedback, data, and conversations that live inside their walls and never reach the audience.

This framing works across all verticals: corporate, government, nonprofit, defense, healthcare.

### The Output

The gap between external perception and internal reality is the brief. The SITREP tells the client: here's how the world sees you (with evidence), here's how you see yourself, here's the gap, here's the story you should be telling and why.

This deliverable is the sale. Production becomes the obvious next step.

### Three Phases (as presented publicly)

**01 — Ingest:** Internal data, audience intelligence, public sentiment, market signals, competitive positioning. Then wider: geopolitical factors, industry shifts, emerging narratives. Everything that shapes how your story will land.

**02 — Synthesize:** LLMs on the full data set. Blind spots in communications, trust gaps with your audience, narrative opportunities your competitors missed. The output: the single highest-leverage story your organization should tell right now.

**03 — Execute:** Real environments, real people, observational production. Technically demanding where the story demands it. Distribution plan built alongside production — not tacked on after delivery.

**Key rule:** The SITREP is built into every engagement. You can't buy production without it.

### SITREP Deliverables (what the client receives)

1. **Intelligence Brief** — Full landscape analysis. Your communications, your competitors, your audience's trust gaps. Where you're winning, where you're losing, and what nobody is saying.
2. **Story Recommendation** — The single highest-leverage story your organization should tell right now. With data backing every creative decision.
3. **Production + Distribution Plan** — Full creative brief, shot plan, and distribution strategy. Who needs to see this, where, and how we reach them. Built alongside production, not after.

## Sales & Pricing

### Entry point

Paid discovery product: $5K–$10K SITREP brief. Layer 1 (OSINT) can run before the client is formally engaged. This is the wedge offer.

### Organizations that typically spend $50K–$500K on video annually

Most of that spend underperforms — not because production quality is bad, but because the strategic foundation is missing. The wrong story, told to the wrong audience, through the wrong channel.

### Sales analogy

Wall Street spends billions on sentiment analysis to figure out what the crowd believes before making a trade. We do the same thing before making media. Smart money doesn't guess. Neither do we.

### Go-to-market model (Russell Brunson framework)

Teach the principle first (clarity precedes craft, you can't tell a true story without knowing what's true), then introduce SITREP as the mechanism. The audience believes in the principle before they ever hear the pitch.

### Lead sales opener

"Every production company asks what you want to make. We show up knowing what you should make."

### Qualifying questions

Ask about past video investment and what it produced. Use the cocktail party version: "We look at everything before we touch a camera..."

### Common prospect triggers

- History of spending on video with no clear return
- Got burned by a previous agency that delivered generic work
- Big moment coming (funding, launch, acquisition, event) with narrative stakes
- Competitor's media is sharper and they're losing ground
- Leadership asking what the marketing budget produced
- Internal disagreement about what the message should be
- Complex organization that's hard to explain to outsiders
- Growth has outpaced the brand

### Pain framework (internal, not public-facing)

**Pain of presence:** Something they have and don't want. Misunderstanding, wasted budget, reputation misalignment.

**Pain of absence:** Something they don't have and need. Visibility, audience understanding, a story that reaches the right people.

We address pain that already exists. We do not install problems in people's heads.

## AI + Human: The Balance

The first half of the client engagement is intelligence-driven. Research agents, data processing, sentiment analysis, pattern recognition. AI handles the scale.

The second half is human. Taste, judgment, artistic vision, emotional calibration, creative risk-taking, documentary filmmaking. AI surfaces what's hidden. Humans decide what it means and make something that moves people.

**The locked-in framing:**

"We built an intelligence platform that scans the entire public web. Audience sentiment, competitive positioning, search behavior, news, social commentary. At a scale no human team could match. It surfaces what's hidden. But making something that actually moves someone? That's still human work."

This is not a tech company that happens to film. It's not a production company that happens to use AI. It's both, fully committed to both, and the combination is the differentiator.

### What the human layer includes

- Real filmmakers with years of experience making people pay attention
- Documentary approach: capturing what's actually happening, not staging it
- Taste and judgment about what to include, what to leave out, what to emphasize
- Emotional precision: making the audience feel something specific
- Creative decisions that no algorithm produces
- Stories about real human beings with something at stake

## Brand Aesthetic

### Direction: Retro sci-fi meets analog warmth

The team are millennials who grew up in the 90s. Clients are typically 80s kids. The aesthetic speaks to that shared visual language.

### Visual references

- **Cinema:** Blade Runner, Alien, Tron, Ex Machina, Arrival. Grainy anamorphic glass. Letterboxed frames. Crushed blacks with warm practicals.
- **Physical media:** VHS tapes, VCR hardware, CRT monitors, film grain, scanlines. The glow of a power LED in a dark room.
- **Feeling:** An intelligence briefing projected onto a CRT in a dark room.

### Color

- **Background:** Near-black (#07070A to #111114)
- **Panel/cards:** Dark gray (#111114 to #161619)
- **Primary accent:** Orange (#FF6B00). The warm amber-orange of a VCR power indicator
- **Text:** Warm white (#E8E0D4). Never cold blue-white
- **Secondary text:** Gray (#777777)
- **Borders:** Subtle dark (#1C1C20)
- **No rainbow on the website.** Rainbow lives in the logo mark and pre-roll animation only

### Typography

- **IBM Plex Mono:** System layer. Navigation, labels, phase numbers, section headers, CTAs, UI elements. Technical precision.
- **Source Serif 4:** Storytelling voice. Headlines, body copy, thesis statements, pull quotes. Human warmth and editorial authority.
- The pairing mirrors the company: machine intelligence (mono) + human craft (serif).

### Texture

- Film grain overlay (subtle, always present)
- Scanline effect (very subtle, CRT reference)
- Ambient radial glows (orange, very low opacity)
- Never flat black. Always atmospheric depth

### Page temperature

The top of the page should feel analytical and precise. The bottom should feel warm and human. The shift happens at the craft section. This mirrors the process: intelligence first, then humans take over.

## Case Study: ITCA (Inter Tribal Council of Arizona)

Three engagements, three subjects. Each required pre-production intelligence that changed the creative direction.

**WIC Nutrition Services:** ITCA needed to increase WIC enrollment. Participation declining despite eligible families. Intelligence revealed tribal members associated WIC with government surveillance — barrier was trust, not awareness. Produced: tribal mothers speaking direct to camera, no narration, trust-first framing.

**Tribal Education Center (TEC):** New facility needed positioning beyond a building tour. Intelligence revealed what mattered was sovereignty over education — decades of federal boarding school trauma. Produced: reframed from facility tour to sovereignty story.

**Voter Engagement:** Low tribal voter turnout across Arizona. Intelligence surfaced historical context: suppression history, redistricting, poll access barriers. Had to confront that history first. Produced: "Your Voice, Your Power." Ten episodes. Tribal leaders, elders, young voters. In production.

**Takeaway:** Three engagements, three subjects. Each required pre-production research that changed the creative direction. We knew what to make before we picked up a camera. That's the SITREP.
